# Google Play Store — App Market Intelligence Report

**Prepared by:** Om Patil  
**Dataset:** Google Play Store (Cleaned)  
**Analysis Period:** 2010 – 2018  
**Report Date:** June 2026

---

## Executive Summary

This report presents a data-driven analysis of **9,462 applications** across **48 categories** on the Google Play Store. The objective is to identify key factors that drive app installs, uncover high-opportunity market segments, and provide actionable recommendations for app developers and product strategists.

The analysis reveals that pricing strategy, content audience targeting, and category selection are the three most decisive factors in determining an app's install volume. Apps that are free, target a mid-age audience (10–17 years), and operate in the gaming vertical consistently outperform the market average.

## 3. Pricing Strategy

### 3.1 Free vs. Paid Performance

| App Type | App Count | Share | Average Installs |
|---|---|---|---|
| Free | 8,742 | 92.3% | 8,504,577 |
| Paid | 734 | 7.7% | 65,588 |

> **Key finding:** Free apps generate **130× more installs** on average than paid apps. This is the single largest performance gap in the entire dataset. Monetisation strategies that rely on in-app purchases or subscriptions — rather than upfront pricing — are strongly supported by this data.

### 3.2 Subscription-Based Apps

| Model | Average Installs |
|---|---|
| Non-subscription (free/one-time) | 8,504,577 |
| Subscription-based | 65,588 |

Subscription apps follow the same pattern as paid apps — significantly fewer installs. This does not mean subscriptions are unviable; rather, subscription products typically rely on a smaller, more committed user base with higher lifetime value.

## 5. Rating & Quality

### 5.1 Average Installs by Rating Group

| Rating Range | Average Installs |
|---|---|
| < 2.0 | 91,741 |
| 2.0 – 3.0 | 154,249 |
| 3.0 – 4.0 | 3,752,232 |
| 4.0 – 4.5 | 10,576,492 |
| 4.5 – 5.0 | 5,508,340 |

> **Key finding:** The sweet spot for installs is a rating between **4.0 and 4.5**, not a perfect 5.0. Apps with perfect ratings tend to have very few reviews, which reduces their credibility and discoverability. A rating in the 4.0–4.5 range, supported by a large review volume, signals both quality and popularity — the combination the Play Store algorithm rewards most.

### 5.2 Correlation with Installs

| Feature | Correlation with Installs |
|---|---|
| Reviews | **0.63** (strong positive) |
| Size | 0.15 (weak positive) |
| Rating | 0.05 (negligible) |

Reviews show the strongest relationship with installs of any numeric feature in the dataset. This reflects a virtuous cycle: apps with more installs accumulate more reviews, which improves visibility, which drives further installs. Review acquisition strategy is therefore a primary growth lever.

## 9. Strategic Recommendations

### For App Developers

**1. Go free, monetise later.**
The data is unambiguous — upfront pricing is the single biggest barrier to installs. Launch free with in-app purchases or a freemium model. Introduce paid tiers only after establishing an install base.

**2. Target the gaming vertical.**
Arcade, Casual, Action, and Strategy apps consistently generate the highest install volumes with relatively low app count competition. Developers with the capacity to build games should prioritise this vertical.

**3. Build for the 10–17 age group.**
"Everyone 10+" and "Teen" content rating apps dramatically outperform other audience segments. Products designed for this cohort — educational games, casual entertainment, social tools — have a structural advantage.

**4. Invest in review acquisition.**
Reviews are the strongest correlating factor with installs. Implement in-app prompts to solicit reviews after positive user moments. A review acquisition strategy is as important as the product itself.

**5. Target a 4.0–4.5 rating, not perfection.**
Focus on consistent user satisfaction rather than chasing a 5.0 score. A 4.2 rating with 50,000 reviews outperforms a 5.0 with 12 reviews — in both credibility and algorithmic ranking.

**6. Time major launches for July–August.**
Summer releases show significantly higher average installs. Plan major version launches, marketing pushes, or new app submissions for June–August to capture peak user activity.

### For Product Strategists

- Avoid entering the Tools or Education category without a strong differentiation strategy — both are overcrowded with low average install returns.
- Communication apps (WhatsApp-like tools, messaging utilities) show strong install performance and remain a viable growth category.
- Medical and Business apps have high app counts but low install averages — niche but potentially high-value per user.

---

## 10. Limitations & Caveats

- **Install data is discretised** — values are bucketed (e.g. 10,000, 50,000, 100,000) rather than exact counts, which limits precision.
- **Dataset covers 2010–2018** — the Play Store landscape has evolved significantly since 2018; findings on category trends may not fully reflect the current market.
- **Revenue not included** — installs are a volume metric. High-install apps in free categories may generate less revenue than lower-install paid or subscription apps.
- **Duplicate entries** — some apps appear multiple times across update snapshots; `.max()` was applied to avoid inflation in top-app analysis.
